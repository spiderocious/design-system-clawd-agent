Dipstick. A station logbook, made disciplined.
LEDGER PAPERBACK · TUE 14 MAY 26

For the Nigerian filling-station owner who counts every drop and every naira, in two, four, or ten branches.

Dipstick is a clean digital logbook. The manager records the day — opening dip, attendant sales, deliveries, expenses, closing dip. The owner sees every branch from one screen, every morning. No POS integrations, no hardware. Just the paper-book the cashier was already keeping, made shared, signed, and unable to be tampered with after the fact.

— Built for the moment the owner asks "why is this short by ₦2,400?" and someone has to know.

Principles of the system
01
Numbers are the loudest object

Litres, naira, meter readings — all tabular monospace. The page should read like a clean ledger column, not a dashboard. Big totals are big. Variance is colour-coded only when it matters.

02
Hairlines, not shadows

Sheets sit on the desk with a 1px paper-edge. No drop-shadows, no cards-on-cards. When separation is loud, we draw an ink rule — never a gradient, never a glow.

03
Green is for posted entries only

The one accent is deep emerald. It marks: signed, balanced, confirmed. Red is reserved for shortages and voids. Everything else stays ink.

04
Scenes, not catalogues

The shift-close, the morning roll-up, the tanker offload — these are the surfaces that matter. State variants live inside real situations, not five-up grids.

05
Posted is final

When a shift is posted, it's signed. Edits leave a visible audit trail. Voiding requires the critical-modal idiom — irreversible, explicit, typed-confirmation. Trust is built on this discipline.

06
Nigerian by default

Naira, litres, NUPENG, AGO/PMS/DPK, NNPC, tanker waybills. The vocabulary is local because the work is. No internationalisation theatre.
